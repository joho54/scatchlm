Chapter 3: Verb Conjugation

The present tense in English is used to describe habitual actions, general truths, and states of being. Understanding these patterns is essential for correct subject-verb agreement.

Regular verbs follow a predictable pattern: I walk → He walks. I study → She studies. When a verb ends in a consonant + y, we drop the y and add -ies.

Irregular Verbs

Some verbs do not follow the standard conjugation rules. These are called irregular verbs. Common examples: to be → I am, you are, he is. To have → I have, she has.

The verb "to be" is the most irregular verb in English. It has unique forms for almost every subject pronoun, and its past tense forms (was, were) are also irregular.

Modal Verbs

Modal verbs (can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must) do not change form for third person singular. They are always followed by the base form of the main verb.

She can swim. He must go. They should study. Note: "ought to" behaves similarly but requires "to" before the base form.

Present Continuous

The present continuous (also called present progressive) is formed with be + verb-ing. It describes actions happening right now or temporary situations.

I am reading. She is writing. They are studying. Spelling rules for -ing: drop silent e (make → making), double final consonant after short vowel (run → running).

Past Simple

Regular past tense is formed by adding -ed to the base form. Pronunciation varies: /t/ after voiceless sounds (walked), /d/ after voiced sounds (played), /ɪd/ after t/d (wanted).

Irregular past tense forms must be memorized: go → went, see → saw, take → took, write → wrote, speak → spoke, begin → began.

Present Perfect

Formed with have/has + past participle. Used for: experiences (I have visited Paris), changes (She has grown taller), continuing situations (We have lived here for 5 years).

Key distinction: I went (past simple, finished) vs I have gone (present perfect, relevant now). American English often prefers past simple where British English uses present perfect.
